Aircraft Investigations — Non‑Offense Workflow

Purpose

This document defines how Aircraft Investigations should be handled in OTIS 2.0. These cases include airplane or helicopter crashes, incidents on runways, or other aviation violations where OSP has jurisdiction or assists another agency.

Key Points

- FAA Numbers & External Investigation: Aviation incidents often have associated FAA investigation numbers and may involve multiple agencies (FAA, NTSB, airport authority). OTIS should provide fields to capture FAA and other external report numbers.
- Photos & Media: Photos and videos are essential evidence for aviation investigations. OTIS must allow uploading photos and linking them to the case. Consider a dedicated “Photo Pouch Number” or metadata field to identify sets of photos.
- Aircraft Crash Report Form: Current practice uses a Microsoft Word Aircraft Crash Report. Users fill out the form and send it to the FAA without necessarily logging a case in OTIS. To improve record‑keeping, OTIS 2.0 should:
  - Offer an electronic version of the Aircraft Crash Report form that can be completed within the system.
  - Allow uploading the completed Word/PDF as an attachment when the form is filled out outside OTIS.
  - Associate the form with the case for future retrieval and public records requests.
- Case vs. Non‑Case: Some minor aviation incidents do not result in an OTIS case, particularly when the FAA or airport authority handles everything. However, a case should be created whenever OSP personnel respond, evidence is collected, or documentation is generated. Even if the only purpose is to store the crash report for future reference, OTIS provides a reliable repository.

Recommended Workflow

1. Incident Creation: If OSP is involved in responding to an aircraft incident, create a non‑offense case with nature code Aircraft Investigations / Violations (870). Add additional nature codes as necessary.
2. Case Details: Record location, date/time, FAA investigation number, and agency contacts (airport authority, NTSB, FAA representatives).
3. Evidence: Upload photos and videos. Enter metadata such as “photo pouch number” if used. Document where evidence is stored.
4. Forms: Launch the Aircraft Crash Report form within OTIS. Complete the form electronically or attach a scanned version. Ensure it captures information required by the FAA, such as aircraft type, tail number, pilot information, and crash circumstances.
5. Narrative: Write a narrative describing trooper actions, coordination with other agencies, and any evidence collected.
6. Disposition: Once FAA and/or NTSB investigation concludes, update the case with final findings if provided and close the case. Maintain the report and attachments for public records.

Notes

- Creating an OTIS case for every aircraft investigation improves record‑keeping and ensures key documents are not lost. It also facilitates public records requests and internal auditing.
- Future enhancements could include integration with FAA/NTSB systems or automated import of crash data.
