OTIS 2.0 Journey Map: Recovered Property (Non‑Offense)

Scenario A: Found Wallet Turned In by Citizen

This journey map describes the experience of an Ohio State Highway Patrol trooper when a traveler turns in a lost wallet at a rest area. It focuses on the human journey through the process (bright spots, pain points, and why each moment matters) rather than on technical requirements or system details.

Purpose

This map helps project teams understand the emotional and practical steps a Recovered Property Reporting Officer goes through when handling recovered property. It highlights the moments that matter most to the officer and identifies bright spots to preserve and pain points to address. Use it to prioritize improvements, design targeted interventions, and measure user experience.

Persona

Recovered Property Reporting Officer

The trooper who first receives recovered property. They are responsible for initial intake, documentation, case creation, HB‑28 completion, narrative creation, and securing the property in temporary storage.

Journey Map (Revised)

Below, each stage of the officer’s journey includes bright spots (positive aspects), pain points (frustrations), and why this moment matters (importance or outcome). Metrics or quotes are provided where relevant.

Stage 1: Property Turned In

- Bright Spots:
- The finder willingly hands over the wallet and provides basic context.
- The trooper has a clear procedure for accepting recovered property.
- Pain Points:
- The finder may refuse to identify themselves or provide inaccurate details.
- The trooper may not receive enough information about where, when, or how the wallet was found.
- Initial intake may be rushed or happen in a public area with distractions.
- Why this moment matters:
- It establishes the chain‑of‑custody; accurate details (finder identity, location, date/time) help identify the rightful owner and reduce misplacement.
- Sets the tone for the rest of the process – early mistakes can be hard to correct later.
- Getting the finder’s contact information enables follow‑up or return if needed.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “I received the property and recorded the finder’s information without causing a delay.”

Stage 2: Initial Assessment

- Bright Spots:
- The trooper can quickly examine the contents (cash, cards, ID) and determine whether the wallet appears to be non‑offense recovered property.
- Standard guidelines exist for distinguishing non‑offense property from criminal evidence.
- Pain Points:
- It may be unclear whether the wallet is connected to a crime; the trooper must decide between creating a recovered property case or an offense case.
- Unknown substances or suspicious items could require additional testing or involve other agencies.
- The trooper may lack a private area to evaluate the property safely.
- Why this moment matters:
- Proper classification ensures the correct workflows (non‑offense vs. criminal) and influences the case’s complexity and ownership.
- Timely assessment prevents property from sitting unattended or untracked.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “I knew immediately which nature code to choose and could proceed with confidence.”

Stage 3: Case Creation

- Bright Spots:
- The trooper has access to OTIS and can create a case in the field.
- Standard nature codes and forms guide the officer through case creation.
- Pain Points:
- Creating a new case may require navigating multiple screens and fields.
- Uncertainty about which nature code to apply (e.g., Recovered Property vs. Lost Property) can cause delays.
- There may be limited connectivity or time pressure to complete the case before another call.
- Why this moment matters:
- The case is the record of truth that downstream actors depend on. Incomplete or inaccurate case creation can disrupt the chain‑of‑custody and cause rework.
- It triggers subsequent workflows (HB‑28, narrative, storage) and ensures accountability.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “Case creation took less than five minutes and captured all required data.”

Stage 4: Property Documentation & HB‑28 Completion

- Bright Spots:
- Troopers know to use the HB‑28 form to document property and record the chain‑of‑custody.
- The form captures key details: type of property, quantity, descriptors, serial numbers, and storage location.
- Pain Points:
- Documentation may be manual or require re‑entry into multiple systems (HB‑28, OTIS).
- Troopers may be unsure whether to itemize each card or lump the wallet contents together.
- Missing or illegible data on HB‑28 can cause delays or require correction by the custodian.
- There is no autosave; losing work due to navigation mistakes or session timeouts is frustrating.
- Why this moment matters:
- Complete documentation ensures property can be tracked and returned to the rightful owner.
- It preserves evidentiary integrity if the wallet later becomes part of a criminal investigation.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “My documentation clearly described the wallet and cash, and the HB‑28 was accepted without corrections.”

Stage 5: Narrative Creation

- Bright Spots:
- Troopers are encouraged to write narratives that provide context, circumstances, and decisions made during intake.
- Narratives help downstream actors understand the situation and any follow‑up actions.
- Pain Points:
- Narratives may be time‑consuming and are often typed in free‑form text areas without autosave.
- If the trooper navigates away accidentally, the narrative may be lost and must be retyped.
- Troopers may struggle to decide how much detail to include; too much or too little can both be problematic.
- Why this moment matters:
- A clear narrative creates transparency, helps maintain the chain‑of‑custody, and can protect the officer from accountability issues.
- Narratives add vital context for property custodians, investigators, and courts.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “I wrote the narrative once and never had to redo it due to system errors.”

Stage 6: Temporary Storage

- Bright Spots:
- Troopers have access to secure temporary lockers at the post.
- There are clear protocols for packaging, labeling, and placing property into storage.
- Pain Points:
- Troopers may not always know which locker is appropriate or available.
- If labels are incorrect or missing, property can be misplaced or require investigation.
- Unclear guidelines on who is responsible for checking the property into the evidence system can cause confusion.
- Why this moment matters:
- Securing the property prevents theft or tampering and maintains chain‑of‑custody.
- Proper labeling ensures that the custodian can quickly locate and remove the wallet for the next step.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “I placed the wallet in the correct locker with a clear label and recorded the location.”

Stage 7: End of Shift & Handoff

- Bright Spots:
- There is a defined handoff process from trooper to custodian; the case and property are ready for the next actor.
- Supervisors can review the case and documentation for completeness before closing the shift.
- Pain Points:
- If a shift ends abruptly, there may not be time to complete documentation or ensure a proper handoff.
- The trooper may not know whether the custodian has received the property, leading to follow‑up calls or uncertainty.
- Cases may remain open if documentation is incomplete, creating more work for the next shift.
- Why this moment matters:
- A clean handoff ensures that the property lifecycle continues smoothly and the trooper is not called back for corrections.
- It reinforces accountability and prevents property from languishing in temporary storage.
- Sample Metric / Quote:
  “I handed off the case and property to the custodian with no outstanding tasks.”

⸻

Key Moments That Matter

These stages are pivotal because they influence whether the officer’s experience is seamless or frustrating. They represent opportunities to create delight or introduce friction:

- Accepting custody and gathering finder information – sets the chain‑of‑custody foundation.
- Classifying and creating the case – determines the workflow path and complexity.
- Documenting property and writing the narrative – preserves accuracy and context.
- Securing property and handing off – ensures continuity for downstream actors.

Measuring ease, emotion, and efficiency at each of these moments helps gauge whether OTIS improvements truly make a difference in troopers’ day‑to‑day work.
